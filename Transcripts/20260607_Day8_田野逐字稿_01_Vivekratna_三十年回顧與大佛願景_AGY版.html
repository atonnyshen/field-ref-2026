第一部分：英文逐字稿 (English Transcript - Verbatim)

Interviewer: Thank you, Vivekratna. I heard you already joined this project in the early '90s?

Lokamitra: Yeah, yeah.

Interviewer: Before becoming a core member of this Triratna Buddhist Community and the director of Nagaloka, what specific life experiences or turning points led you to leave a conventional path and dedicate yourself entirely to Dr. Ambedkar's Buddhist revival movement?

Lokamitra: Oh, yes. In a way, it is a very long story, but I will make it as brief as possible.

Interviewer: No, no, if it is long, it's okay, if you don't mind.

Lokamitra: I was living in a small village, and all my high school education was in that small village. After matriculation, I came to Nagpur, and I used to visit Deekshabhoomi. Since we were calling ourselves Buddhists—because when Babasaheb converted to Buddhism at Deekshabhoomi in 1956, the whole Mahar community of Maharashtra, whether they officially took the refuges and precepts from any monk or not, started calling themselves Buddhists.

Interviewer: So, you are Mahar?

Lokamitra: Yes, I belong to the Mahar community. The whole Mahar community within Maharashtra state were calling themselves Buddhists. If someone called them by their old caste names, there would be a lot of agitation and violent disputes happening.

Interviewer: Oh, right. There were disputes because they were now calling themselves Buddhists.

Lokamitra: Yes. "Why are you calling us by our old names? We are not by that name anymore." That was the situation at that time. We called ourselves Buddhists, but we didn't know anything about Buddhism. That was the only thing.

Interviewer: Yes.

Lokamitra: But we changed our identity from the old Hindu religion and the old caste to being Buddhist. We were very proud to be Buddhists, even though we didn't know anything about the practice of Buddhism.

Interviewer: You didn't know how to practice, but you already believed in it.

Lokamitra: Yes, yes. After coming to Nagpur and frequently visiting Deekshabhoomi, I met a very renowned Theravada monk, Anand Kausalyayan, who used to stay in Nagpur at that time.

Interviewer: Oh, a renowned monk?

Lokamitra: Yes, he was a Theravada monk, but he was very famous in India and a highly literary person. He used to stay at Deekshabhoomi and give lectures on full moon days and during other events. We used to go there, bow down to the monks, respect them, and listen to their paritrana patha recitations. That was going on.

At that time, political and religious movements were combined. The Republican Party of India established by Babasaheb and the Bharatiya Bauddh Mahasabha (the Buddhist Society of India) were working together. The political leaders were also the religious leaders. Because of that combination, there was no proper Dhamma education or real practice.

When this was going on, I first encountered Triratna. I read Sangharakshita’s book, The Ideal of Human Enlightenment, and I realized that just being a Buddhist and leaving everything in the hands of the monks—believing they are the real Buddhists and we are just their supporters—was not enough.

Interviewer: Yes, it's like in ancient India, where the laity just supports the monks.

Lokamitra: Yes. It made us feel like second-class Buddhists, a lower grade, while the monks were the higher grade. We saw that difference very clearly. Monks had to be highly respected, even if their behavior was not always up to the mark. That was the mindset. We believed we couldn't practice ourselves because we were laypeople.

But when I came into Triratna and read Sangharakshita's The Ideal of Human Enlightenment, things changed. At the time, I was working for BSNL and was transferred from Nagpur to Mumbai. In Mumbai, I attended a lecture and a retreat led by Padmavajra. I was very impressed. He was a disciple of Sangharakshita and was working with Lokamitra to establish Triratna in India.

Hearing Padmavajra express Dr. Ambedkar's vision and explain his book The Buddha and His Dhamma was unlike anything I had ever heard before, even up to the age of 35. I had never heard such explanations from any political leader or monk. I was deeply attracted to it.

During that retreat, he taught us meditation—Anapanasati (the Mindfulness of Breathing) and Loving-Kindness (Metta Bhavana). He explained that even as householders, we can practice a committed Buddhist life and follow the ten precepts. That turned my life around. I maintained continuous contact with the movement.

Through Padmavajra, I met Lokamitra for the first time at a meeting in Mumbai. After Padmavajra returned to the UK around 1990, I became an Order member in 1988, and Lokamitra continued to guide and educate us in those early days. That was the beginning of my journey.

Interviewer: How old were you then?

Lokamitra: I became an Order member in 1988 at the age of 40. I came into contact with Triratna around 1986, when I was 38, and became an Order member two years later.

Interviewer: So you were still quite young and energetic.

Lokamitra: Yes, I was 40. After joining the Order, I involved myself more and more in its activities, starting as a local chapter convener. In 1991, we took the lead to establish a Triratna center in Nagpur.

Interviewer: In 1991? But you hadn't acquired this land yet, right?

Lokamitra: No, not yet. In September 1991, we officially established the Nagpur Triratna Center.

Interviewer: Was there an invitation, or did the center just pop up?

Lokamitra: It just popped up. We started doing social work and organizing retreats. At that time, we organized Sangharakshita's visit to Nagpur for January 1992. We worked very hard because we only had five or six Order members in Nagpur.

With Lokamitra's inspiration, we started Balwadis (preschool community centers) in the slum areas. We established five Balwadis back then. We also started a men's community and created an Arts and Sports Center. The arts program included singing, mono-acts, and street plays to educate people on what it truly means to be a Buddhist.

Interviewer: To help more people understand.

Lokamitra: Yes, to reach more people. Since we were working with youth, we also started karate classes. Amoghasiddhi, who is now an Order member, was the leader of that karate group. Through karate practice, many young people ended up joining Triratna.

We brought youth to Buddhism through karate, singing, and cultural activities.

Interviewer: I see. It was a way to make it easier for people to get in touch with Buddhism.

Lokamitra: Yes, it made it very accessible. We started that center before Bhante (Sangharakshita) visited in January 1992. His visit was grand. We organized his lecture on a massive scale at Kasturchand Park, and nearly 75,000 people attended. It was a highly inspiring and spirited period for us.

We worked all over Nagpur. I was leading three large weekly Dhamma classes myself.

Interviewer: What kind of classes?

Lokamitra: Weekly classes to teach the Dhamma. Nearly 200 people attended regularly. Around that time, we invited Padmavajra from the UK to stay with us for two years to inspire the people and lead lecture series. It was wonderful.

Interviewer: Is Padmavajra a teacher or a doctor?

Lokamitra: He is a teacher. The doctor was Virabhadra, who started the medical project in Dapoli.

After establishing our first Triratna Center in Nagpur in 1991, our activities expanded. We started a boys' hostel in 1989 and a girls' hostel in 1992.

Interviewer: So Triratna's entry into Nagpur was actually later than in Pune?

Lokamitra: Yes. I was the first Order member from Nagpur, and before that, there was no Triratna presence in the city. When I lived here, I was initially a member of the Wardha chapter. Wardha is near Nagpur. Have you been to Bordharan to see the retreat center?

Interviewer: No, but I plan to go. I would like to visit the Hsuan Tsang Retreat Centre because it is an important place.

Lokamitra: Yes, you should. Venerable Hsin Tao has a very deep emotional attachment to that center and donated significantly toward it. I think Kuang Shin also contributed, though his major contribution went to the Nagarjuna Training Institute.

The Wardha center was established in 1987, and they started a boys' hostel there. After I was transferred from Bombay to Nagpur, I joined the Wardha center as a participant and traveled there from Nagpur to join their activities. Through the Wardha center, I eventually became an Order member.

In 1989, Lokamitra visited Taiwan. During his visit, he saw an opportunity to establish a retreat center in our area. When he returned, we looked at the land, and the retreat center was built. Venerable Hsin Tao inaugurated the center in 1992 during its first phase.

Our teacher, Venerable Sangharakshita, was also present for the inauguration because he was visiting Nagpur in January 1992. Lokamitra organized Venerable Hsin Tao's visit at the same time so they could inaugurate the center together.

Interviewer: That meditation center is very nice.

Lokamitra: Yes, that is how it all started. Then around 1992 and 1993, Venerable Kuang Shin, Dr. Yo (Prof. Yo Hsiang-Chou), and Lokamitra had a dialogue. They resolved to help us create the Nagarjuna Training Institute in Nagpur. We found and purchased this land in 1994.

Interviewer: And you were fully engaged in this project from the start?

Lokamitra: Fully engaged. Since 1994, I have been completely dedicated to this project—securing the land, planning the buildings, and managing the operations.

First, we built the Buddha Surya Vihara, which is the Dhamma Hall. Immediately after, we constructed the Nagarjuna Training Institute building and began our activities. Our vision was to bring poor youth from all over India, especially those who believe in Dr. Ambedkar's vision, and train them here. We wanted to introduce them to who Dr. Ambedkar really was—not just as a political icon for "Jai Bhim" slogans or as the father of the Indian Constitution, but what he truly did for his community, his country, and the revival of Buddhism.

It is very important for the youth to understand Dr. Ambedkar's vision and basic Buddhism, which we teach here at the Nagarjuna Training Institute. When our students graduate and become alumni, we want them to do social and Dhamma work in their own areas.

Even without large financial resources to start big projects like hostels, they can work at an awareness level. They can address issues like domestic violence, drug addiction, smoking, and other social challenges that are prevalent in slum areas.

Interviewer: We see those slums everywhere.

Lokamitra: Yes. We train them to raise awareness about how these habits harm individuals, families, and society, and how to overcome them. Even simple acts, like giving free tuition to poor children who cannot afford school, are valuable forms of social work and community interaction. We encourage our youth to do this.

This was the vision behind the institute, and in 2002, we established the Nagarjuna Training Institute as a registered charitable trust. We purchased the land in 1994 and inaugurated the campus in 1995. Venerable Hsin Tao and Dr. Yo visited, and we named the campus Nagaloka.

In 1996, the first Dhamma Hall was completed, followed by the training building in 1997. Later, we built the residential building, Adhisthana, for the boys to stay. During that period, we used the training building for Pali classes, seminars, and workshops.

In 2002, we established the Nagarjuna Training Institute trust and started the residential training course. At that time, it was a ten to eleven-month course.

Interviewer: Now it is eight months, but back then it was almost a full year?

Lokamitra: Yes, it was essentially an eleven-month program. It started on Dhamma Chakra Pravartan Day (around July 1st) and ended around Buddha Purnima (the full moon of Buddha's enlightenment in May). Depending on the year, some batches stayed for exactly ten months and others for eleven.

I ran those courses from 2002 to 2011. For those nine years, I focused entirely on training, teaching, interacting with students, and coordinating with alumni. I organized alumni conferences, retreats, and worked to keep them inspired. Slowly, I withdrew from direct administration, and since 2016, I have been concentrating on expanding Dhamma activities across the rest of India through our alumni network.

Interviewer: Where do these alumni work?

Lokamitra: We are working across ten states, including Bihar, Uttar Pradesh, Rajasthan, Madhya Pradesh, Chhattisgarh, Telangana, Karnataka, Kerala, Maharashtra, and Gujarat.

Interviewer: Mainly in central and southern India?

Lokamitra: Yes, because those areas have historically had less access to these teachings. We also have students from West Bengal and Arunachal Pradesh, such as Chakma Buddhists, who come from traditional Buddhist backgrounds. Many boys and girls from the Chakma community in Arunachal Pradesh have trained here. One Chakma boy actually runs our bookshop at the campus entrance. Have you met him?

Interviewer: No, but I met Bhanu.

Lokamitra: Bhanu is from Rajasthan, in northwestern India.

Interviewer: And I met Sangharsh, though I don't know where he is from.

Lokamitra: He is from Bhandara, which is very close to Nagpur.

Interviewer: I see. It's incredible that students come from all over the country. I saw the billboard showing that students have come from 26 different states. Nagaloka is a relatively small place, yet its reach is nationwide.

Lokamitra: Yes, that residential training is the absolute core of Nagaloka. In 2013, we also started a college offering a BA degree.

Interviewer: A BA degree program?

Lokamitra: Yes. The focus of the college is to provide higher education in Buddhist Studies, Dr. Ambedkar's thoughts, and the Pali language. This degree is affiliated with and awarded by Nagpur University.

Interviewer: So you recruit teachers, design the curriculum, and manage the administrative operations?

Lokamitra: Yes, we handle everything. The reason we started the college was that some students who completed the eleven-month residential course wanted to stay longer to deepen their Dhamma practice and help develop this place. Since they were already staying for two years, we thought that if they stayed for one more year, they could earn a BA degree.

We approached Nagpur University, and they were willing. Then we got approval from the government. In the early years, the college followed an annual examination pattern, which worked very well. But after two years, the university shifted to a semester system.

Because of the semester pattern, the depth of training we wanted to provide in Ambedkar's thoughts and Buddhism has become somewhat diluted, as we have to align with the university's standardized program. In our ten-month training program, we cover these subjects intensely. Under the BA program, that same content is spread out over three years.

Interviewer: But on the positive side, it gives them more time to reflect and practice.

Lokamitra: Yes, they have more time to practice and contemplate. We are very proud that several of our graduates have gone on to find good jobs, some have become lecturers, and some have completed their PhDs.

Interviewer: Do some of them stay here to teach or manage the center?

Lokamitra: Yes. One of our current teachers is a former student from Odisha. He first did the Dhammasekhiya course, then completed his BA here, and later went to the Wardha Central University to get his PhD. He just submitted his thesis and is now teaching Buddhist Studies here. We have several students like him.

Interviewer: It has been almost a thirty-year journey for the campus, and you have been here the whole time.

Lokamitra: The college started in 2013, so that has been thirteen years. But yes, for the campus as a whole, it has been over thirty years. It has been a long journey.

Interviewer: Looking back at the 1990s when you first started grassroots work in Nagpur, the conditions must have been very tough. During those days of going door-to-door and scrambling for resources, what were your daily interactions with the local community like? How did you divide the labor?

Lokamitra: To be honest, I felt incredibly inspired back then.

Interviewer: Many of the Mitras I talked to used that exact word: "inspired."

Lokamitra: Yes, inspired. I was so inspired that I don't even know where all that energy came from. Looking back, it seems almost beyond imagination how we managed in such difficult times. It was miraculous that we were able to gather 75,000 people for Sangharakshita's lecture.

The entire community in Nagpur, especially the youth, was full of energy. They went door-to-door collecting small donations and dedicating their time.

Back then, we had no mobile phones or social media. We had to paste paper posters on street walls and buses to advertise our events. Financial resources were extremely limited. Communication with Lokamitra was done through letters, weekly reports sent by post, or occasional phone calls. Since I worked at BSNL, I had easier access to telephone lines, but I still wrote weekly reports by hand and mailed them.

Interviewer: How long did it take for a letter to arrive?

Lokamitra: It took about three to four days, and then another few days to get a reply. It was a weekly cycle of communication. Things started changing slowly after 2000. We got our first office computer around 1999.

When we purchased this land, we set up an office in the city. Since I was working full-time at BSNL, I couldn't be there during the day, so we had another Order member running the office. Later, Maitreyanath came to Nagpur and took charge of the social projects, including the hostels, Balwadis, and the Arts and Sports Center. He managed the social side while I focused on the Dhamma side, giving lectures and conducting classes.

Maitreyanath served as the executive director and I was the chairperson. He kept in close contact with Lokamitra, and we worked together as a very harmonious and consistent team.

We knew we were building something significant.

Interviewer: Can you recall a specific instance where you were deeply inspired by Lokamitra?

Lokamitra: In July 1995, a year after we purchased this land, I attended an Order convention in the UK. It was my very first time traveling abroad. When I saw England—their infrastructure, lifestyle, culture, and facilities—I thought, "This place is like heaven." Compared to that, India felt incredibly difficult to live in.

While I was in the UK, my daughter had to be hospitalized back in India. To get updates, I had to use a public coin box, dial the international number, and keep feeding coins just to speak to my family. Communication was so difficult back then; even calling from one city to another within India was a struggle.

When I returned from the UK, I had a conversation with Lokamitra during his visit to Nagpur. I asked him, "Lokamitra, England is like heaven in terms of material comfort, and India is so difficult with the roads, the dirtiness, the food, and the living conditions. What made you leave all that comfort to stay here and commit your entire life to teaching the Dhamma?"

He didn't give me a complex philosophical answer. He just smiled and said, "I was young, and it was a challenge I wanted to take on."

His answer made me reflect deeply. I thought, "Here is a Westerner who left an easy life to come here, eat simple food like pohe and bhakri with his bare hands, and live without proper facilities for the sake of Babasaheb's Dhamma revolution. If he can dedicate his entire life to this work, what am I doing as a follower of Babasaheb and as a native Buddhist?" That realization changed my entire perspective. I realized I had to support him and this work at any cost. From that point on, I became far more serious and dedicated myself fully to this mission.

Interviewer: That was in 1995?

Lokamitra: Yes, right after we purchased the land. That conversation made me leave all my hesitation behind and simply do whatever was needed to support him.

Interviewer: You have built and managed a very beautiful campus here. The staff and students are so focused on their practice.

However, when two people with strong visions work together, disagreements are inevitable. During the establishment and management of Nagaloka, what major issues did you, Lokamitra, and the other core team members differ on the most? How did you ultimately reach a consensus? I know you spent a lot of time living and working closely together.

Lokamitra: Yes. Our primary shared vision has always been to spread Buddhism and carry forward Babasaheb's Dhamma revolution as taught by Sangharakshita. That was always the foremost priority for all of us. But when it came to implementation, we sometimes had different opinions.

For example, take the large walking Buddha statue, which is now an iconic landmark of Nagaloka. The initial idea for a statue came from Venerable Kuang Shin from Taiwan, who visited us and suggested we needed a grand Buddha statue here, promising to support it. However, a couple of years later, he found it difficult to fund the project.

Lokamitra still wanted to push forward with the statue. While I supported him, several other Order members in India were strongly against the idea. They argued that the funds required for the statue could be better used to establish smaller Dhamma centers across the country.

Lokamitra believed that once we installed a grand Buddha statue, it would attract thousands of visitors whose donations would make Nagaloka financially self-sufficient, meaning we wouldn't have to constantly rely on external fundraising.

I did not agree with that specific assumption. I told him, "This is not going to work that way in India with our local community." And time has proven me right. The donations from the visitors do not generate enough money to even cover the maintenance of the lawns and gardens. People come to see the statue, but very few donate.

Even though I disagreed with his financial assumption, I fully supported his vision that we must have a majestic Buddha statue here to represent the Dhamma. I expressed my difference of opinion honestly, but once we made the decision, I worked whole-heartedly to make it happen. That is how we operated—we discussed our differences openly, but we always moved forward together.

Interviewer: To the outside world, Lokamitra is seen as a highly energetic and indefatigable leader. In your decades of working together, have you ever seen moments of vulnerability or deep frustration in him, perhaps due to political pressures or lack of funds? How did you support each other during those times?

Lokamitra: Yes, I have seen him deeply frustrated, particularly regarding the legal issues with our land transfer.

Because the Nagarjuna Training Institute did not have an FCRA (Foreign Contribution Regulation Act) registration in its early days, the funds donated from Taiwan for purchasing the Nagaloka land had to be sent to the TBMSG Pune trust, which already possessed FCRA clearance. Consequently, the land was registered under the name of TBMSG Pune.

Once Nagaloka grew and established its own identity, we wanted to transfer the land title from TBMSG Pune to the Nagarjuna Training Institute.

The process was extremely slow and legally complicated. After we finally secured approval from the Charity Commissioner's office for a 30-year lease transfer, the Indian Ministry of Home Affairs suddenly changed the FCRA regulations. Under the new rules, land purchased using FCRA funds by one trust could no longer be transferred to another trust.

It was an incredibly frustrating and helpless situation for Lokamitra. The land and several buildings remain registered under TBMSG Pune, while some newer buildings are under the Nagarjuna Training Institute. It created a legal mess that is still at a standstill today.

Interviewer: How does Lokamitra deal with that kind of frustration?

Lokamitra: He is a remarkable person in that regard. He often uses the metaphor of flowing water. He says that when water flows and meets an obstruction, it doesn't just stop; it finds a way to go around, over, or under the obstacle.

He doesn't dwell in a frustrated mood for long. He is highly intelligent and quick to adapt, constantly looking for alternative ways to solve a problem.

Interviewer: When you first stood on this site, it was just agricultural land. What did you and Lokamitra envision for its future? How did the physical transformation of the campus—from the first building to Christopher Benninger's designs inspired by the Ajanta Caves—alter the psychological landscape of the Dalit Buddhist movement?

Lokamitra: Before we purchased this land, Lokamitra had a strong friendship with Christopher Benninger, one of India's leading architects. Benninger had already designed the Dapodi Mahavihara, the Jivaka center, and our girls' hostel in Pune.

After buying this land, we walked the fields together—myself, Lokamitra, and Christopher. Christopher created a master plan with different phases because we had to build gradually as funds became available.

Lokamitra's friend, Dr. Yo, played a key role in introducing Lokamitra to various Buddhist monasteries and lay donors in Taiwan who contributed generously. If you look at the foundation stones around the campus, you will see many Taiwanese names, alongside other dignitaries like His Holiness the Dalai Lama and Venerable Hsing Yun.

Christopher designed the buildings, and Lokamitra and I discussed and approved them. I didn't have a background in architecture, so I generally trusted their judgment, though I occasionally gave feedback on practical details.

The first structures we built were the Dhamma Hall (Buddha Surya Vihara) and the training building. At first, we just had a general idea of creating a grand Dhamma center that would serve as a spiritual capital for the modern Buddhist revolution in India.

When the training building was completed in 1997 and we began occupying it in 1998, we started planning its daily usage systematically. By 2001, we decided to launch a formal residential training program, which meant we needed a residential hostel.

We also realized that while the Buddha Surya Vihara was open to the public, our resident trainees needed a quiet, dedicated space for intensive meditation, which led to the construction of the meditation hall.

We also realized that when visitors came to the campus, they needed an immediate, clear symbol of what this place stood for. That is why we installed the walking Buddha statue at the center, so that anyone entering the campus would immediately know they were in a sacred Buddhist space.

We also commissioned a unique statue of Dr. Ambedkar. Most statues of Dr. Ambedkar in India depict him in a standard political pose, pointing a finger. But since Nagaloka is a center for spiritual and social transformation, we wanted a statue that captured his expression at the moment of his historic conversion—dignified, walking forward, and looking toward a new future.

From the beginning, my main concern was that Buddhism in India should not become associated with only one specific caste. It needed to be all-embracing. We wanted to bring youth from different states and caste backgrounds—from Odisha, South India, the North, and the West—and help them transcend their caste identities through living and practicing together.

Under the traditional caste system, people are taught from childhood to see themselves as superior or inferior based on their birth. But when they come to Nagaloka, live together, and study together, they realize that caste is nothing but a mental construct.

They experience a casteless society firsthand, which is exactly Dr. Ambedkar's vision.

Over the years, the campus developed further. We built the Upaya building for alumni activities, and later, the girls' hostel. Previously, the female trainees had to stay at a separate hostel in the city, which was a 20-minute auto-rickshaw ride away. They had to commute back and forth, and they lacked direct access to the library and campus facilities. So, we constructed the girls' hostel directly on campus to ensure their safety and integration.

Although we have a beautiful campus now, we face a major frustration because the government currently does not allow us to construct any new buildings on this site.

Interviewer: Yes, I heard from Dr. Munjal that you received funds for expansion but cannot build here due to government restrictions.

Lokamitra: Yes. With the donor's permission, we used those funds to purchase a new 43-acre plot of land nearby. We plan to develop that land into a retreat center and an agricultural training facility. When one door closes, we find another way.

Interviewer: Last night, I went for a walk and saw the students doing evening meditation with their teacher. They described a very strict daily routine: waking up at 5:30 AM, attending two long meditation sessions, maintaining strict gender segregation, keeping silence, and using no mobile phones. What is the educational philosophy behind such a disciplined, offline environment? What qualitative changes do you hope this discipline will bring about in these young minds?

Lokamitra: I designed and established this disciplined routine in the early days of the institute. While some rules have been relaxed over the years, the core structure remains.

In India, young women are often socially vulnerable. When we bring girls from different remote parts of India to Nagpur, we must assure their parents and guardians that they are completely safe and secure here. Maintaining strict boundaries between the boys and the girls is essential for building that trust with their families.

Additionally, our students are mostly between 18 and 25 years old. At that age, their minds can easily get distracted. The ten-month training period is very short, and we want them to focus entirely on transforming their minds and absorbing the Dhamma without distractions.

Today, mobile phones are like a drug and cause constant distraction. When I enforced the "no phone" policy, I faced a lot of criticism, but I stood firm because deep focus is essential for genuine mental transformation.

Our routine of morning meditation, study, manual work, and evening chanting is designed to build teamwork and break down old prejudices. In India, manual labor is traditionally looked down upon and associated with lower castes. People often carry this Brahminical attitude of wanting others to do the hard work for them.

We teach our students that working with their hands and cleaning their own living spaces, including toilets, is an essential part of Buddhist practice. It humbles the mind and builds a true sense of equality.

We maintain a structured schedule: waking up early, meditating, attending classes, participating in group discussions, working together, chanting, and going to bed by 10:00 PM.

Interviewer: When I met the students yesterday, they seemed incredibly mindful and polite, which is quite different from many youths outside.

Lokamitra: The students you met yesterday are our BA degree students who have already been here for two or three years, which is why you see that maturity in them. When new students first arrive, it is very different. Many of them find the discipline hard and want to leave within the first few days.

We give them a 15-to-20-day grace period to adjust to the environment. We tell them, "Don't make a hasty decision. Stay for two weeks, and if you still find it too difficult, you are free to leave." On average, we have about a 20% dropout rate each year.

We don't force anyone to stay; they must decide for themselves. We explain that this is not a training program for securing a job or financial benefit, but a life training program to transform their character, attitude, and outlook toward the world.

Interviewer: Currently, over 60% to 70% of the Nagaloka staff—like Bhanu, Anand, Varsha, Rajkumari, and Renuka—are your former students. Even the team at the Upaya building, like Priti, Christopher, and Paul, are ex-students. How does it feel to see the operations being taken over by the younger generation you helped cultivate? How does their understanding of Dr. Ambedkar's engaged Buddhism differ from that of your pioneering generation?

Lokamitra: Yes, almost all of our key staff members are alumni of our program. Some have gone on to complete higher education, like Bhanu, who completed his Master's in Social Work from the prestigious Tata Institute of Social Sciences, and others who are now lecturing in our college. They are highly educated and deeply devoted to carrying Babasaheb's mission forward.

Interviewer: After decades of struggle, if you were to sit down for a cup of tea with Dr. Ambedkar today, what would you both consider your unfinished business? What are your deepest concerns and greatest hopes for the future of Nagaloka and Triratna?

Lokamitra: The future of Nagaloka depends entirely on commitment—commitment to Dhamma practice and commitment to carrying Dr. Ambedkar's social and spiritual mission forward. Without that deep personal commitment, Nagaloka has no real future.

The future of Triratna is effectively the future of the Dhamma revolution in India. We are not interested in just building empty temples or having people call themselves Buddhists while continuing to worship old Hindu gods and goddesses, which Dr. Ambedkar explicitly discarded in his 22 vows. Within the Triratna community, we have successfully moved away from those old practices.

That is why the Nagarjuna Training Institute is so critical; we are shaping the minds of the next generation of leaders. To sustain this, we need committed individuals at every level of our organization, from top management to the grassroots staff. I sometimes feel a bit uneasy about whether we can find enough people with that level of selfless commitment today.

Interviewer: You are currently traveling all over the country to support the alumni network. Is that your main focus of unfinished business right now?

Lokamitra: Yes, that is my primary focus. I will be turning 78 next month, and Lokamitra is ten months older than me; he will turn 79 in September. Vivekratna：是的。我們目前的一位老師是來自奧里薩邦（Odisha）的前學生。他首先在這裡學習了法法（Dhammasekhiya）課程，然後完成了文學士學位，之後前往瓦爾達中央大學獲得了博士學位。他剛剛提交了論文，現在在這裡教授佛教研究。我們有幾位像他這樣的學生。

訪談者：對這個校園來說，這已經是將近三十年的旅程了，而您一直都在這裡。

Vivekratna：學院始於2013年，所以那是十三年。但是是的，對於整個校區來說，已經有三十多年了。這是一段漫長的旅程。

訪談者：回顧1990年代您最初在那格浦爾開展基層工作時，當年的條件一定非常艱苦。在那些挨家挨戶走訪、艱難籌集資源的日子裡，您與當地社區的日常互動是怎樣的？您們是如何分工的？

Vivekratna：坦白說，那時我感到無比地受到鼓舞。

訪談者：我交流過的許多法友（Mitras）也都用了同一個詞：「受到鼓舞」（inspired）。

Vivekratna：是的，受到鼓舞。我是如此受到鼓舞，以至於我都不知道所有這些能量是從哪裡來的。回想起來，在如此艱難的時期，我們是如何度過難關的，簡直超乎想像。我們能為僧護導師的演講聚集7.5萬人，這簡直是個奇蹟。

那格浦爾的整個社區，特別是年輕人，都充滿了活力。他們挨家挨戶地募集微薄的捐款，並奉獻自己的時間。

那時候，我們沒有手機，也沒有社交媒體。我們必須把紙質海報貼在街頭牆壁 and 公共汽車上來宣傳我們的活動。資金非常有限。與世友居士的溝通是透過信件、郵寄每週報告，或偶爾打電話來進行的。因為我在國營電信公司工作，我比較容易使用電話線，但我仍然手寫每週報告並郵寄出去。

訪談者：一封信要花多長時間送達？

Vivekratna：大約需要三到四天，然後再過幾天收到回信。這是一個每週一次的溝通循環。2000年以後，情況開始慢慢發生變化。我們在1999年左右有了第一台辦公電腦。

當我們購買這片土地時，我們在市區設立了一個辦公室。因為我在電信公司全職工作，白天不能待在那裡，所以我們由另一位戒體成員來管理辦公室。後來，梅特里亞納特（Maitreyanath）來到那格浦爾，負責社會項目，包括寄宿學校、幼兒園以及藝術和體育中心。他管理社會事務，而我則專注於佛法方面，進行演講並開班授課。

梅特里亞納特擔任執行主任，我擔任主席。他與世友居士保持密切聯繫，我們作為一個非常和諧、一致的團隊共同工作。我們知道我們正在建設一些非常重要的事情。

訪談者：您能回憶起一個您被世友居士深深鼓舞的具體例子嗎？

Vivekratna：1995年7月，也就是我們購買這片土地的一年後，我前往英國參加了戒體大會。這是我第一次出國旅行。當我看到英國——他們的基礎設施、生活方式、文化和設施時，我想：「這個地方簡置就像天堂。」相比之下，印度顯得無比艱難。

當我在英國時，我的女兒在印度不得不住院。為了獲取最新消息，我必須使用公共投幣電話亭，撥打國際號碼，並不斷投幣才能與家人通話。那時的溝通是如此困難，甚至在印度國內不同的城市之間通話都很吃力。

當我從英國回來後，世友居士訪問那格浦爾時，我和他進行了一次談話。我問他：「世友居士，就物質舒適度而言，英國就像天堂，而印度在道路、髒亂、食物和生活條件方面是如此艱苦。是什麼讓您放棄了所有這些舒適，留在這裡並將您的一生奉獻給弘揚佛法？」

他沒有給我一個複雜的哲學回答。他只是笑了笑說：「那時我很年輕，這是我想要接受的挑戰。」

他的回答讓我陷入了深深的反思。我想：「這是一位西方人，他放棄了安逸的生活來到這裡，用手吃著像波黑（pohe）和巴克里（bhakri，印度大餅）這樣簡單的食物，在沒有妥善設施的情況下生活，就為了巴巴巴薩海布的佛法革命。如果他能為這項工作奉獻他的一生，那我作為巴巴巴薩海布的追隨者和本土的佛教徒，又在做什麼呢？」那次省思改變了我整個視角。我意識到，無論付出什麼代價，我都必須支持他和這項工作。從那時起，我變得更加認真，全力以赴地投入到這個使命中。

訪談者：那是在1995年嗎？

Vivekratna：是的，就在我們購買土地之後。那次談話讓我拋開了所有的猶豫，只要是支持他所需的事情，我都毫無保留地去做。

訪談者：您在這裡建立並管理了一個非常美麗的校園。工作人員和學生都非常專注於他們的修行。

然而，當兩個擁有強烈願景的人一起工作時，分歧是不可避免的。在建立和管理龍世學園的過程中，您、世友居士以及其他核心團隊成員在哪些重大問題上的分歧最大？您們最終是如何達成共識的？我知道您們花了大量時間在一起密切地生活和工作。

Vivekratna：是的。我們共同的首要願景始終是傳播佛教，並繼承僧護導師所教授的巴巴巴薩海布的佛法革命。這對我們所有人來說始終是最優先的考量。但到了具體實施時，我們有時會有不同的意見。

例如，以那座巨大的弘化大佛雕像為例，它現在是龍世學園標誌性的地標。建造雕像的最初想法來自台灣的廣心上人，他訪問我們時建議我們這裡需要一座宏偉的佛像，並承諾給予支持。然而，幾年後，他發現很難為這個項目提供資金。

世友居士仍然想推進雕像項目。雖然我支持他，但印度其他幾位戒體成員強烈反對這個想法。他們認為，建造雕像所需的資金可以用來在全國各地建立較小的佛法中心。

世友居士相信，一旦我們安座了一尊宏偉的佛像，它將吸引成千上萬的訪客，他們的捐款將使龍世學園在財務上實現自給自足，這意味著我們不必一直依賴外部籌款。

我並不同意這個具體的設想。我告訴他：「這在印度和我們的當地社區是行不通的。」時間證明我是對的。訪客的捐款甚至不足以支付草坪和花園的維護費用。人們來看雕像，但很少有人捐款。

儘管我不同意他的財務設想，但我全力支持他的願景，即我們必須在這裡安座一尊莊嚴的佛像來代表佛法。我坦率地表達了我的不同意見，但一旦我們做出了決定，我就全心全意地去實現它。這就是我們的運作方式——我們公開討論分歧，但我們始終攜手前行。

訪談者：對外界來說，世友居士被視為一位精力充沛、不知疲倦的領袖。在您們幾十年的合作中，您是否曾見過他脆弱或深感挫折的時刻，也許是由於政治壓力或缺乏資金？在那些時期，您們是如何互相支持的？

Vivekratna：是的，我曾見過他深感挫折，特別是關於我們土地轉讓的法律問題。

由於龍樹學院在早期沒有 FCRA（國外捐款管理條例）註冊，台灣為購買龍世學園土地而捐贈的資金不得不寄給已經擁有 FCRA 許可的浦那印度佛教普濟會（TBMSG）（TBMSG Pune trust）。因此，這片土地是以浦那 TBMSG 的名義註冊的。

一旦龍世學園成長並確立了自己的身分，我們就希望將土地所有權從浦那 TBMSG 轉讓給龍樹學院。

這個過程非常緩慢且在法律上極其複雜。在我們終於獲得慈善專員辦公室批准進行30年租約轉讓後，印度內政部突然修改了 FCRA 法規。根據新規定，一個信託利用 FCRA 資金購買的土地，再也無法轉讓給另一個信託。

這對世友居士來說是一個令人無比沮喪和無能為力的境地。這片土地和幾座建築目前仍然登記在浦那 TBMSG 名下，而一些較新的建築則在龍樹學院名下。這造成了法律上的混亂，至今仍處於停滯狀態。

訪談者：世友居士是如何應對這種挫折感的？

Vivekratna：在這方面他是一個非常了不起的人。他經常使用流水來作比喻。他說，當水流動並遇到阻礙時，它不會就此停止；它會尋找方法繞過、越過或從障礙物下方穿過。

他不會在沮喪的情緒中停留太久。他非常聰明，適應能力極強，總是能尋求解決問題的其他替代途徑。

訪談者：當您第一次站在這個地方時，這裡還只是農田。您和世友居士對它的未來有什麼設想？校園的物理轉變——從第一棟建築到克里斯多福·貝寧格（Christopher Benninger）受阿旃陀石窟（Ajanta Caves）啟發的設計——是如何改變達利特（Dalit）佛教運動的心靈版圖的？

Vivekratna：在我們購買這片土地之前，世友居士與印度頂尖建築師之一克里斯多福·貝寧格有著深厚的友誼。貝寧格已經設計了達波迪大寺（Dapodi Mahavihara）、吉瓦卡（Jivaka）中心以及我們在浦那的女童寄宿學校。

買下這片土地後，我們一起在田野間漫步——我、世友居士和克里斯多福。克里斯多福制定了一個分階段的總體規劃，因為我們必須隨著資金的到位而逐步建造。

世友居士的朋友游教授在向台灣的多個佛教寺院和在家居士捐助者介紹世友居士方面發揮了關鍵作用，他們慷慨解囊。如果您看校園各處的奠基石，您會看到許多台灣人的名字，以及尊者達賴喇嘛和星雲大師等其他顯貴的名字。

克里斯多福設計了這些建築，世友居士和我進行討論並予以批准。我沒有建築背景，所以我基本上信任他們的判斷，儘管我偶爾會對實用細節提出反饋。

我們建造的第一批結構是佛堂大廳（大雄寶殿 (Buddha Surya Vihara)）和培訓大樓。起初，我們只有一個大致的想法，即建立一個宏偉的佛法中心，作為印度現代佛教革命的精神首都。

當培訓大樓於1997年完工，我們於1998年開始使用時，我們開始系統地規劃其日常用途。到2001年，我們決定啟動正式的寄宿制培訓項目，這意味著我們需要一棟宿舍樓。

我們也意識到，雖然大雄寶殿 (Buddha Surya Vihara)是對公眾開放的，但我們的常住學員需要一個安靜、專屬的空間來進行密集禪修，這促成了禪堂的建造。

我們還意識到，當訪客來到校園時，他們需要一個直接、清晰的象徵來表明這個地方代表著什麼。這就是為什麼我們在中心安座了弘化大佛雕像，這樣任何進入校園的人都會立刻知道他們置身於一個神聖的佛教空間。

我們還委託製作了一尊獨特的安貝卡博士雕像。在印度，安貝卡博士的大多數雕像都描繪成標準的政治姿勢，用手指著前方。但由於龍世學園是一個精神與社會轉型的中心，我們想要一尊能夠捕捉他在歷史性皈依那一刻表情的雕像——莊嚴、向前邁步並展望新的未來。

從一開始，我最關心的是，印度的佛教不應該只與某個特定的種姓聯繫在一起。它需要是包容一切的。我們希望引進來自不同邦和種姓背景的青年——來自奧里薩邦、南印度、北部和西部——並幫助他們透過共同生活和修行來超越自己的種姓身分。

在傳統的種姓制度下，人們從小就被教導根據出生來判定自己是優越還是低賤。但當他們來到龍世學園，生活在一起，學習在一起時，他們意識到種姓不過是一種觀念上的建構。

They 體驗到了一個無種姓的社會，這正是安貝卡博士的願景。

多年來，校園得到了進一步的發展。我們建造了用於校友活動的「方便大樓」（Upaya building），隨後又建造了女童宿舍。以前，女學員必須住在市區一個獨立的宿舍裡，乘三輪摩托車需要20分鐘的車程。她們必須來回通勤，且無法直接使用圖書館和校園設施。因此，我們直接在校園內建造了女童宿舍，以確保她們的安全和融入。

雖然我們現在擁有一個美麗之校園，但我們面臨著一個重大的挫折，因為政府目前不允許我們在此地建造任何新建築。

訪談者：是的，我聽蒙雅爾博士（Dr. Munjal）說，您們收到了擴建資金，但由於政府的限制而無法在這裡建造。

Vivekratna：是的。在捐贈者的許可下，我們用這些資金在附近購買了一塊新的43英畝土地。我們計劃將那片土地開發為一個禪修中心和一個農業培訓設施。當一扇門關閉時，我們會找到另一條路。

訪談者：昨晚我散步時，看到學生們在和老師一起做晚課禪修。他們描述了一個非常嚴格的日常作息：早上5:30起床、參加兩次長時間的禪修、保持嚴格的性別隔離、保持靜默且不使用手機。在這樣一個紀律嚴明、不聯網的環境背後，有著怎樣的教育哲學？您希望這種紀律能給這些年輕人的心靈帶來怎樣的質的變化？

Vivekratna：在學院創立初期，我設計並確立了這一紀律嚴明的作息。雖然這些年來有些規則有所放寬，但核心結構依然保留。

在印度，年輕女性在社會上往往處於弱勢。當我們把來自印度不同偏遠地區的女孩子帶到那格浦爾時，我們必須向她們的父母和監護人保證她們在這裡是完全安全和有保障的。保持男女學員之間嚴格的界限，對於與她們的家人建立這種信任至關重要。

此外，我們的學生大多在18至25歲之間。在那個年齡，他們的心思很容易受到干擾。十個月的培訓期非常短，我們希望他們完全專注於轉化自己的心靈並吸收佛法，而不受外界干擾。

如今，手機就像毒品一樣，會造成持續的干擾。當我執行「禁用手機」的規定時，我面臨了許多批評，但我堅持立場，因為深度專注對於真正的心理轉化至關重要。

我們清晨禪修、學習、勞動和晚間課誦的作息，旨在培養團隊合作並打破舊有的偏見。在印度，體力勞動在傳統上是被看不起的，並與低種姓聯繫在一起。人們往往帶著這種婆羅門式的態度，希望別人為自己做艱苦的工作。

We 教導我們的學生，動手勞動和清理自己的生活空間（包括廁所）是佛教修行不可或缺的一部分。這能降伏傲慢的心，並建立真正的平等感。

我們保持著條理分明的作息表：早起、禪修、上課、參與小組討論、共同工作、課誦，並在晚上10點前就寢。

訪談者：當我昨天見到學生時，他們顯得非常有正念和禮貌，這與外面的許多青年有很大不同。

Vivekratna：您昨天遇到的學生是我們文學士學位的學生，他們已經在這裡待了兩三年，這就是為什麼您在他們身上看到了那種成熟。當新學生剛來時，情況非常不同。他們中的許多人覺得紀律很難適應，在最初幾天就想離開。

我們給他們15到20天的適應期來融入這個環境。我們告訴他們：「不要草率做出決定。留下來兩個星期，如果您仍然覺得太困難，您可以自由離開。」平均而言，我們每年的流失率大約在20%左右。

我們不強迫任何人留下，他們必須自己做出決定。我們解釋說，這不是一個為了獲得工作或經濟利益的培訓項目，而是一個轉變他們的性格、態度和世界觀的人生修煉項目。

訪談者：目前，龍世學園超過 60% 到 70% 的工作人員——比如巴努（Bhanu）、阿南德（Anand）、瓦莎（Varsha）、拉傑庫馬里（Rajkumari）和雷努卡（Renuka）——都是您的前學生。甚至方便大樓（Upaya building）的團隊，比如普里蒂（Priti）、克里斯多福（Christopher）和保羅（Paul），也都是前學生。看到運營工作被您協助培養的年輕一代接管，您有何感受？他們對安貝卡博士的人間佛教（Engaged Buddhism）的理解，與您們開拓者那一代有何不同？

Vivekratna：是的，我們幾乎所有的關鍵工作人員都是我們項目的校友。有些人已經完成了更高層次的教育，比如巴努（Bhanu），他從著名的塔塔社會科學研究院（TISS）獲得了社會工作碩士學位，還有一些人現在在我們的學院裡擔任講師。他們受過良好的教育，並深切地致力於推動巴巴巴薩海布的使命前進。

訪談者：在經歷了數十年的奮鬥之後，如果您今天能與安貝卡博士坐下來喝杯茶，您們雙方會認為什麼是「未竟的事業」？您對龍世學園和三寶佛教社團未來的最大擔憂和最大希望是什麼？

Vivekratna：龍世學園的未來完全取決於奉獻精神——對佛法修行的奉獻，以及對推動安貝卡博士的社會和精神使命前進的奉獻。沒有那種深沉的個人奉獻，龍世學園就沒有真正的未來。

三寶佛教社團的未來實際上就是印度佛法革命的未來。我們對僅僅建造空洞的寺廟或讓那些繼續崇拜舊印度教神祇（而這些在安貝卡博士的22條誓言中已明確廢除）的人自稱為佛教徒不感興趣。在三寶佛教社群內，我們已經成功地擺脫了那些舊有的習俗。

這就是為什麼龍樹學院如此關鍵；我們正在塑造下一代領導者的心靈。為了維持這一點，我們需要在組織的各個層面上都有具有奉獻精神的人，從高層管理到基層員工。我有時會有些不安，不知道今天我們是否能找到足夠多擁有如此無私奉獻水平的人。

訪談者：您目前正在全國各地旅行以支持校友網絡。這是您目前未竟事業的主要焦點嗎？

Vivekratna：是的，這是我最主要的焦點。下個月我就要滿78歲了，而世友居士比我大十個月，他將在9月份滿79歲。雖然世友居士仍擁有令人難以置信的體能，但我正在竭盡全力，用我僅存的精力來加強全印度的校友網絡。

我們的校友面臨的最大挑戰是財務上的生存。印度普遍存在失業問題，我們的許多年輕人正面臨著供養家庭的巨大壓力。當他們在為日常生存而掙扎時，很難讓他們專注於一個更大的社會事業。

要運行這樣一個運動，我們需要願意過簡單、奉獻生活的人。僧護導師教導了我們一種受大乘理想啟發的奉獻實踐：簡單的生活、無生涯規劃（careerlessness）、更少的個人財產、共享的社區以及對佛法的奉獻。今天很難找到體現所有這些品質的人。這就是為什麼我從喀拉拉邦旅行到拉賈斯坦邦，從北方邦到安得拉邦，試圖尋找並培養這樣有奉獻精神的人。

我最關心的問題是財務的可持續性。我們需要能夠支持我們的全職工作者，以便至少滿足他們的基本生活需求。由於我們的捐贈資金有限，世友居士在過去幾年裡一直努力在我們新的農業土地上發展商業項目，以產生可持續的收入來支持校園。

訪談者：我希望這些努力能夠成功，並壯大您在這裡的優秀團隊。非常感謝您，Vivekratna，接受這次漫長且令人振奮的訪談。

Vivekratna：謝謝。我很高興我們進行了這次對話。希望我們還能再見面。
